# Chapter 5 — Knowledge Sources and Seeds

A startup may agree with Knowledge Before Generation and still face an obvious problem: it has little organizational knowledge to capture.

It has no mature component library, no years of research, and no established design language. It may have a logo, a handful of screens, and strong preferences such as “use Lucide,” “follow WCAG 2.2 AA,” or “apply the usability principles from *Don't Make Me Think*.”

That is enough to begin.

AINDS distinguishes between **knowledge sources** and **seeds**.

Knowledge sources are trusted bodies of guidance an organization chooses to adopt. They may include accessibility standards, usability books, design references, content style guides, engineering conventions, or an established icon family.

Seeds are concrete starting materials that reveal preference or intent:

- A favorite product or website
- Brand assets
- Existing screenshots
- A selected typeface
- An icon family
- A few approved illustrations
- A reference component library
- A preferred interaction pattern

Seeds are not yet a design system. They are evidence from which one can infer candidate rules.

Suppose a founder says, “We like this application.” A weak process copies its appearance. A stronger process asks what specifically is valued: density, hierarchy, tone, restraint, navigation model, spacing, or content clarity. Those observations become explicit hypotheses that humans can approve.

Likewise, choosing Lucide does more than select SVG files. It establishes a visual source with conventions that govern future additions. Choosing WCAG 2.2 AA establishes an accessibility baseline. Choosing a content style guide establishes defaults for casing, terminology, and grammar.

The organization does not need to invent best practices. It needs to document:

1. The source
2. What it is adopting
3. How it interprets the source locally
4. Known exceptions
5. Ownership and review

Over time, adopted sources and seeds evolve into organizational knowledge. The goal is not permanent imitation. It is a deliberate starting point.

> **Seeds are the starting point. Knowledge is the destination.**

## Key takeaways

- Greenfield organizations can begin without a mature design system.
- Trusted external sources reduce the need to invent every practice.
- Seeds reveal preferences but require interpretation and approval.
- AINDS turns selected sources into explicit local decisions.
